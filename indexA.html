Who is Muhammad Ali?

Muhammad Ali was an American professional boxer and social activist. A global cultural icon, widely known by the epithet "The Greatest", he is frequently cited as the greatest heavyweight boxer of all time.


Quick Facts

  • Full Name: Muhammad Ali (born Cassius Marcellus Clay Jr.)
  • Born: January 17, 1942, Louisville, Kentucky, USA
  • Died: June 3, 2016, Scottsdale, Arizona, USA
  • Nickname: "The Greatest," "The Louisville Lip"
  • Height: 6'3" (191 cm)
  • Weight Class: Heavyweight
  • Professional Record: 56 wins, 5 losses, 37 knockouts

Early Life:

Ali began boxing at the age of 12, after his bike was stolen, and was mentored by trainer Joe Martin. He won a gold medal in the light heavyweight division at the 1960 Summer Olympics in Rome.

Boxing Career:

Ali was known for his speed, footwork, and unique fighting style, often describing himself as "float like a butterfly, sting like a bee."

He became the World Heavyweight Champion three times during his career, which was a remarkable feat in the boxing world.

Some of his most famous matches include: 1964: Defeated Sonny Liston to win the World Heavyweight title for the first time.

1971: Lost to Joe Frazier in the "Fight of the Century," but they had two more legendary encounters.

1974: Defeated George Foreman in the "Rumble in the Jungle" in an unexpected upset.

1975: Won the "Thriller in Manila" against Joe Frazier in one of the most brutal and enduring fights in boxing history.

Cultural and Social Impact:

Ali was a major figure in the civil rights movement and was known for his outspoken views on race, religion, and social justice. He converted to Islam in the 1960s and changed his name from Cassius Clay to Muhammad Ali. He famously refused to fight in the Vietnam War, stating, "I ain't got no quarrel with them Viet Cong," which led to his boxing license being revoked and his title being stripped in 1967. He was later reinstated and returned to boxing after his legal battles.

Personal Life:

Ali was married four times and had nine children. In 1984, he was diagnosed with Parkinson's disease, a condition that affected his health for the rest of his life

Legacy:

Ali is often regarded as the greatest boxer of all time, not just for his accomplishments in the ring but also for his impact outside of it. He was awarded numerous honors, including Sports Illustrated's Sportsman of the Century in 1999. His legacy goes beyond boxing, as he became a symbol of resilience, bravery, and the fight for justice and equality, particularly for African Americans and marginalized communities.
